# **Atomistic Structural Analysis and Computational Reconstruction Framework for Chiral Stationary Phases**

## **Introduction to the Atomistic Modeling of Enantioselective Chromatographic Systems**

The precise computational reconstruction of chiral stationary phases is an increasingly critical endeavor in the fields of physical chemistry, pharmaceutical development, and separation science. As high-performance liquid chromatography and supercritical fluid chromatography remain the gold standards for resolving enantiomeric mixtures, the ability to computationally model and predict these separations *in silico* offers immense value.1 Molecular dynamics and density functional theory simulations provide unparalleled insights into the transient diastereomeric complexes formed between chiral selectors and analyte molecules. However, the validity of these computational models is strictly dependent upon the exact geometric, crystallographic, and chemical accuracy of the reconstructed stationary phase.3

The target chiral stationary phases subjected to this structural verification encompass ten highly utilized commercial columns. Nine of these are polysaccharide-based architectures: Chiralpak AD, Chiralpak AY, Chiralpak IB, Chiralpak IC, Chiralpak IE, Chiralpak IF, Chiralpak IG, Chiralcel OX, and Chiralcel OZ. The tenth is a fundamentally distinct, small-molecule brush-type phase known as Whelk-O 1\. To accurately reconstruct these systems in a simulated environment, one must move beyond the basic chemical formulas and define the supramolecular conformations, the precise helical pitches, the inter-rod packing distances, and the specific chemical linkages anchoring the chiral selectors to the underlying silica matrix.3 This exhaustive report validates the chemical data provided in the target dataset, establishes the exact parameters required for building computational coordinate files, and explores the nuanced physicochemical mechanisms driving chiral recognition across these diverse systems.

## **Supramolecular Architecture of Polysaccharide-Based Chiral Selectors**

The remarkable enantiodiscrimination capabilities of polysaccharide-based chiral stationary phases arise entirely from their highly ordered, conformationally rigid secondary structures. Unlike linear synthetic polymers that adopt random coil conformations in solution, exhaustively derivatized amylose and cellulose polymers fold into rigid, regular helices.3 This helical folding generates a continuous, chiral supramolecular groove along the length of the polymer backbone, which acts as the primary interactive domain for resolving enantiomers. For computational scientists constructing initial coordinate files, understanding the fundamental geometric differences between the amylose and cellulose backbones is the most critical step in establishing the simulation boundary conditions.

### **The Amylose Backbone and the Left-Handed 4/3 Helical Conformation**

Amylose is a highly uniform, linear biopolymer composed of D-glucose monomeric units linked exclusively through ![][image1]\-(1,4)-glycosidic bonds. The stereochemistry of this alpha-linkage imparts a natural, acute angle between adjacent glucose rings, causing the polymer to spontaneously coil into a spring-like helical structure even in its underivatized state. When the hydroxyl groups at the C2, C3, and C6 positions of the glucose rings are fully reacted with phenyl isocyanate derivatives to form carbamates, the resulting steric bulk forces the polymer into a highly specific, left-handed 4/3 helical conformation.2

In a left-handed 4/3 helix, exactly four glucose residues are required to complete three full macroscopic turns of the helical structure. Exhaustive structural analyses utilizing two-dimensional nuclear overhauser effect spectroscopy coupled with molecular mechanics and X-ray diffraction have unequivocally confirmed this geometry for amylose tris(3,5-dimethylphenylcarbamate), the chiral selector in Chiralpak AD and Chiralpak IA.2 For the purposes of atomistic reconstruction, the translation along the central helical axis—defined as the helical pitch—has been experimentally measured at 14.6 Å (1.46 nm).3 The contour length or helix rise per individual glucose residue is approximately 3.6 to 3.8 Å.8

Because the 4/3 helix is tightly wound, the overall diameter of the polymer rod is relatively compact. X-ray diffraction results indicate that when these amylose rods pack together to form the crystalline or semi-crystalline domains responsible for chiral recognition, the inter-rod packing distance is 18.9 Å.3 This inter-rod dimension is of paramount importance for setting the unit cell vectors in molecular dynamics software packages. If multiple amylose rods are arrayed in a simulation box, placing them closer than 18.9 Å will result in artefactual steric clashes and unnatural repulsion forces, while placing them too far apart will fail to replicate the dense, confined interstitial spaces where solvent molecules and analytes frequently interact.3

Furthermore, computational models must account for the mechanical rigidity of these amylose rods. Light scattering and small-angle X-ray scattering studies have determined that the Kuhn segment length—a primary parameter of polymer stiffness—for amylose tris(3,5-dimethylphenylcarbamate) can reach up to 73 nm in certain solvents like 4-methyl-2-pentanone.8 This exceptionally high stiffness is attributed to the steric hindrance of the bulky phenylcarbamate side chains and the extensive intramolecular hydrogen bonding networks that stabilize the helix. Consequently, molecular dynamics simulations can confidently treat these amylose helices as semi-rigid rods, applying position restraints to the main carbohydrate backbone to preserve the 4/3 helical geometry while allowing the phenylcarbamate side chains to freely rotate and sample local conformational phase space.

### **The Cellulose Backbone and the Left-Handed 3/2 Helical Conformation**

Cellulose differs from amylose solely in the stereochemistry of its glycosidic linkage, being composed of D-glucose units connected by ![][image2]\-(1,4)-glycosidic bonds. In nature, the equatorial beta-linkage allows cellulose to form extended, flat, ribbon-like structures that pack tightly into high-tensile-strength microfibrils. However, when the three hydroxyl groups on each glucose unit are exhaustively derivatized with bulky phenylcarbamate groups, the linear ribbon conformation becomes sterically impossible to maintain. To alleviate the severe steric clashes between adjacent functional groups, the derivatized cellulose chain twists into a left-handed threefold 3/2 helical structure.2

In the 3/2 helical geometry, three glucose residues are distributed across two full turns of the helix. This conformation is more elongated and less tightly coiled than the amylose 4/3 helix.3 Crystallographic data, primarily derived from X-ray diffraction of highly crystalline cellulose tris(phenylcarbamate) films and fibers, dictates that the helical pitch of this structure is 16.2 Å (1.62 nm).3 The hard-core diameter of the cellulose rod itself is estimated to be approximately 14.0 Å (1.4 nm).12

Due to the expanded nature of the 3/2 helix and the differing spatial projection of the phenyl rings relative to the central axis, the parallel packing of these polymer rods requires more spatial volume than their amylose counterparts. The experimentally determined inter-rod packing distance for cellulose tris(3,5-dimethylphenylcarbamate), the chiral selector utilized in Chiralpak IB and Chiralcel OD, is significantly wider at 21.0 Å.3 When performing atomistic reconstructions of the target cellulose-based columns—specifically Chiralpak IB, Chiralpak IC, Chiralcel OX, and Chiralcel OZ—the modeler must generate the initial coordinates using a 3/2 rotational translation algorithm and arrange the resulting rods in a periodic lattice spaced at 21.0 Å intervals.3 Failing to transition between the 18.9 Å spacing of amylose and the 21.0 Å spacing of cellulose will result in catastrophic structural anomalies during the isothermal-isobaric equilibration phase of the simulation.

## **The Chiral Helical Groove and Mechanisms of Enantiodiscrimination**

The spatial organization of functional groups along these helical polymer chains is highly conserved and forms the molecular basis for enantioselective chromatography. Regardless of whether the backbone is a 4/3 amylose helix or a 3/2 cellulose helix, the functional moieties arrange themselves into a distinct inside-outside radial pattern.1

The glucose rings themselves constitute the innermost core of the polymer rod. Extending immediately outward from the glucose core are the polar carbamate linkages (-NH-CO-O-). Because these polar groups are geometrically constrained near the backbone, they collectively form a continuous, twisted cleft known as the chiral helical groove.1 This groove is the primary active site of the stationary phase. The amide protons (-NH) project into the groove acting as potent hydrogen bond donors, while the carbonyl oxygens (C=O) act as strong hydrogen bond acceptors and dipole-dipole interacting partners.13

Projecting furthest from the central axis and shielding the polar core are the hydrophobic phenyl rings.1 These aromatic structures line the outer perimeter of the polymer rod, controlling steric access to the inner chiral groove and providing extensive surface area for non-covalent ![][image4]\-![][image4] stacking and hydrophobic interactions with the target analytes.7 For an enantiomer to be successfully retained and resolved, it must physically intercalate into the chiral groove. One enantiomer will typically achieve a highly complementary, three-point interaction encompassing multiple hydrogen bonds and a ![][image4]\-![][image4] stacking event, leading to a strong, enthalpically favorable transient inclusion complex. The opposing enantiomer, due to its mirror-image spatial topology, will experience steric repulsion from the outer phenyl rings or an inability to simultaneously align its hydrogen bonding sites, resulting in a weaker interaction and faster elution.10

## **Substituent Effects on Phenylcarbamate Selectors**

The precise electronic and steric nature of the substituents attached to the outer phenyl rings drastically alters the enantioselectivity of the stationary phase. The dataset encompasses three distinct categories of substitution: dialkyl (dimethyl), dihalogen (dichloro), and mixed halogen-alkyl (chloro-methyl).13 These substitutions modify the chiral recognition environment through inductive electronic effects, resonance stabilization, and targeted spatial occlusion.

### **Electron-Donating Substituents: Dimethylphenylcarbamates**

The Chiralpak AD and Chiralpak IB columns utilize 3,5-dimethylphenylcarbamate substituents on amylose and cellulose, respectively.5 The methyl groups are electron-donating by nature, pushing electron density into the phenyl ring through inductive effects and hyperconjugation. This increased electron density delocalizes toward the carbamate linkage, significantly increasing the partial negative charge on the carbonyl oxygen.1 Consequently, the carbonyl group becomes an exceptionally strong hydrogen bond acceptor. This electronic enhancement, combined with the relatively low steric footprint of the methyl groups, renders the 3,5-dimethyl derivatives highly versatile, establishing them as the standard universal phases for broad-spectrum chiral screening.1

### **Electron-Withdrawing Substituents: Dichlorophenylcarbamates**

The Chiralpak IC and Chiralpak IE columns are functionalized with 3,5-dichlorophenylcarbamate groups.5 Chlorine atoms are highly electronegative and exert a strong electron-withdrawing inductive effect across the aromatic ring. This withdrawal depletes electron density from the carbamate linkage. While this reduces the basicity and hydrogen-bond-accepting capacity of the carbonyl oxygen, it dramatically increases the polarity and acidity of the amide (-NH) proton, converting it into a much stronger hydrogen bond donor.13

Furthermore, the introduction of chlorine atoms necessitates specialized parameterization in computational models. Halogen atoms bonded to aromatic rings exhibit an anisotropic distribution of electron density, creating an area of depleted electron density along the extension of the carbon-halogen covalent bond. This region of positive electrostatic potential is known as a sigma-hole (![][image5]\-hole).18 Sigma-holes are capable of highly specific, directional, non-covalent interactions with Lewis bases, a phenomenon termed halogen bonding.3 Standard classical molecular dynamics force fields, which typically treat atoms as centered point-charges, inherently fail to capture sigma-holes. Therefore, atomistic reconstructions of the IC and IE phases must incorporate off-center, positive dummy atoms positioned on the chlorine vectors, or utilize advanced multipole force fields parameterized via high-level density functional theory calculations (e.g., restricted electrostatic potential charge fitting) to accurately model enantioselective halogen bonding.4

### **Asymmetric Mixed Substituents: Chloro-Methylphenylcarbamates**

The most chemically nuanced columns in the target dataset are those possessing mixed substituents on the phenyl rings: Chiralpak AY (5-chloro-2-methyl), Chiralpak IF (3-chloro-4-methyl), Chiralpak IG (3-chloro-5-methyl), Chiralcel OX (4-chloro-3-methyl), and Chiralcel OZ (3-chloro-4-methyl).16 These derivatives feature a deliberate asymmetry in their electronic distribution, combining the electron-donating properties of a methyl group with the electron-withdrawing properties of a chlorine atom on the exact same aromatic ring.13

This antagonistic push-pull electronic environment induces strong localized dipoles across the phenyl ring, fundamentally altering how the aromatic system interacts with the ![][image4]\-electrons of passing analytes. More importantly, the asymmetrical steric bulk alters the rotational resting state of the phenyl rings relative to the central chiral groove.3 The slight geometric twisting caused by the asymmetric substitution creates uniquely shaped access windows to the carbamate hydrogen bonding sites. This explains why structural isomers—such as the 4-chloro-3-methyl variant (OX) and the 3-chloro-4-methyl variant (OZ)—often exhibit entirely reversed elution orders for specific enantiomers, despite possessing identical chemical formulas and molecular weights.15 Reconstructing these specific phases computationally requires extensive initial energy minimization using steepest descent algorithms to allow the sterically constrained phenyl rings to find their local asymmetric energy minima without shattering the rigid underlying 3/2 or 4/3 carbohydrate backbone.

## **Physical Coating Versus Covalent Immobilization to the Silica Matrix**

The topological definition of the simulated system is highly dependent upon the mode of attachment between the chiral polymer and the underlying mesoporous silica gel matrix. The commercial columns in the dataset are distinctly categorized into "coated" generations and modern "immobilized" generations. This distinction governs not only the laboratory solvent compatibility of the columns but also defines the physical boundary equations required for atomistic molecular dynamics.

### **Non-Covalent Physical Coating (AD, AY, OX, OZ)**

The traditional manufacturing technique for preparing polysaccharide chiral stationary phases involves synthesizing the functionalized polymer in bulk, dissolving it in a compatible organic solvent (such as tetrahydrofuran), and slurry-mixing it with highly purified, wide-pore silica gel.22 Prior to coating, the silica gel is typically treated with 3-aminopropyltriethoxysilane to create a uniform aminopropyl functionalized surface. The solvent is subsequently evaporated under vacuum, leaving the chiral polymer physically adsorbed onto the silica support.23

The target columns utilizing this physical coating architecture are Chiralpak AD, Chiralpak AY, Chiralcel OX, and Chiralcel OZ.5 Because there are no covalent bonds linking the polymer to the silica, the polymer chains are held in place entirely by relatively weak intermolecular forces, including hydrogen bonding between the silica's surface amines and the polymer's carbamate carbonyls, as well as general van der Waals dispersion forces.25 In an experimental setting, this physical adsorption means the columns are highly susceptible to dissolution; exposing a coated column to "forbidden" solvents like chloroform, dichloromethane, ethyl acetate, or pure tetrahydrofuran will rapidly solvate the polymer, strip it from the silica, and irreversibly destroy the column.17

For computational modelers, reconstructing a coated phase is relatively straightforward. The simulation box should consist of an amorphous silica slab terminated with aminopropyl silane chains. The 36-mer or nonamer polysaccharide rods are then positioned adjacent to the silane layer. No covalent topology links are required between the polymer and the surface. During the equilibration phase of the simulation, the polymer rods will naturally adjust their orientation and establish non-covalent contacts with the surface layer, mimicking the physical adsorption process.

### **Covalent Chemical Immobilization (IB, IC, IE, IF, IG)**

To overcome the severe solvent restrictions associated with coated phases, manufacturers developed sophisticated chemical processes to covalently anchor the polysaccharide chains directly to the silica matrix.17 This immobilization confers universal solvent compatibility, allowing chromatographers to utilize highly solvating normal-phase, reversed-phase, and polar organic mobile phases to optimize challenging separations.17

The target columns in the dataset that feature this advanced covalent immobilization are Chiralpak IB, Chiralpak IC, Chiralpak IE, Chiralpak IF, and Chiralpak IG.5 While the exact industrial parameters of Daicel's immobilization process are proprietary, the scientific literature outlines several verified synthetic pathways utilized to achieve this permanent linkage.22

The most prominent and computationally relevant method involves the use of bifunctional cross-linking agents, specifically diisocyanates such as 1,6-hexamethylene diisocyanate (HDI).22 In this procedure, the polysaccharide is partially derivatized, leaving a small fraction of the C6 primary hydroxyl groups unreacted. The polymer is coated onto aminopropyl silica, and the diisocyanate is introduced. One isocyanate group reacts with the primary amine on the silica surface to form a urea linkage, while the other isocyanate group reacts with the residual C6 hydroxyl on the polysaccharide to form a urethane (carbamate) linkage.24 This creates a highly stable, flexible aliphatic tether spanning between the polymer backbone and the silica surface.

An alternative established method involves silanization of the polymer itself. The polysaccharide is reacted with reagents such as (3-glycidoxypropyl)triethoxysilane or triethoxysilylpropyl isocyanate.28 This introduces triethoxysilyl groups directly onto the polymer chain. When mixed with the bare silica support, these groups undergo intermolecular polycondensation with the surface silanols (Si-OH), forming robust, direct siloxane (Si-O-Si) bridges between the chiral selector and the matrix.24 Other documented techniques include the photochemical or radical-initiated cross-linking of vinyl-modified polysaccharides, creating complex carbon-carbon bonded networks.22

For the precise atomistic reconstruction of the IB, IC, IE, IF, and IG phases, the topological coordinate files must contain explicit covalent bonds bridging the polymer and the support. The most chemically representative model utilizes a low-density fractional tethering approach, where approximately one in every ten to fifteen glucose residues features an aliphatic ether or urethane cross-link terminating in a siloxane bond on the amorphous silica slab.24 It is imperative to note that this covalent anchoring fundamentally restricts the conformational mobility of the polymer. Immobilization inherently distorts the local helical geometry at the specific anchor points, preventing uniform swelling. This structural distortion explains why an immobilized column (e.g., Chiralpak IB) often exhibits slightly different enantiomeric retention times and selectivities compared to its exact coated counterpart (e.g., Chiralcel OD) when operated under identical mobile phase conditions.26

## **The Pirkle-Type Brush Phase: Whelk-O 1 Architecture**

The final entry in the target dataset, Whelk-O 1, requires a completely different computational paradigm. Developed by William Pirkle and commercialized by Regis Technologies, Whelk-O 1 is not a helical polymer. It belongs to the class of "brush-type" or low-molecular-weight chiral stationary phases.33 The active selector is a discrete, single molecule: 1-(3,5-dinitrobenzamido)-1,2,3,4-tetrahydrophenanthrene.

Because it is a small molecule, geometric variables such as "helical pitch," "inter-rod packing distance," and "helix type" are fundamentally non-applicable to Whelk-O 1\. The chiral recognition mechanism does not involve the intercalation of analytes into a supramolecular groove. Instead, Whelk-O 1 operates via a highly localized, rigid molecular cleft that features two distinct, complementary interactive faces.34 The 3,5-dinitrobenzoyl moiety provides a strongly ![][image4]\-acidic (electron-deficient) face, while the tetrahydrophenanthrene ring system provides a robust ![][image4]\-basic (electron-rich) face.34

When an enantiomeric analyte enters this cleft, discrimination is achieved through a strict, three-point interaction model. This typically involves a ![][image4]\-![][image4] donor-acceptor stacking event, a targeted hydrogen bond at the central amide linkage, and steric wedging against the rigid phenanthrene backbone.34 If the enantiomer possesses the correct absolute configuration, it perfectly satisfies all three interaction sites, resulting in strong retention. The opposite enantiomer cannot simultaneously align its corresponding functional groups without inducing severe steric clashes, leading to rapid elution.

The covalent immobilization of Whelk-O 1 to the silica support is engineered to maximize the accessibility of this cleft. The selector is grafted to the silica utilizing a long aliphatic silane tether—most commonly a 10-undecenyl or 3-propyl linkage.33 Crucially, the linkage to the chiral selector occurs at a secondary amino group.33 This specific secondary amine attachment is a deliberate design choice; it avoids the presence of an additional amide hydrogen at the tether point, which Pirkle identified as a cause of non-stereoselective retention and diminished column efficiency.33

To reconstruct the Whelk-O 1 phase *in silico*, the modeler must bypass polymer generation entirely. The system should be built by generating an amorphous silica surface and attaching a dense self-assembled monolayer (SAM) of the 10-undecenyl or 3-propyl tethers, capped with the 1-(3,5-dinitrobenzamido)-1,2,3,4-tetrahydrophenanthrene selector.33 The extended aliphatic chain acts as a flexible spacer, elevating the bulky chiral cleft away from the surface silanols to prevent analytes from bypassing the selector and non-specifically adsorbing to the bare silica.38

## **Solvation Dynamics, Swelling, and Thermodynamic Modeling**

In computational chromatography, the stationary phase cannot be modeled accurately in a vacuum. The mobile phase solvent actively participates in the chiral discrimination process, altering both the structural conformation of the stationary phase and the energetic landscape of the cavity. Experimental evidence utilizing attenuated total reflection infrared spectroscopy (ATR-IR) and solid-state NMR confirms that polysaccharide CSPs undergo significant solvent-induced swelling.4

When immersed in a standard normal-phase eluent composed of highly non-polar hexane and a polar alcohol modifier (e.g., 2-propanol or ethanol), a phase separation occurs at the microscale. The bulky hexane molecules predominantly occupy the interstitial voids between the parallel polymer rods, providing bulk volume but participating in minimal direct interactions. Conversely, the polar alcohol modifiers penetrate deeply into the chiral helical groove, actively competing with analytes for hydrogen bonding sites at the carbamate linkages.4 The absorption of these polar solvents dictates a reorganization of the intra- and inter-rod hydrogen bonds.

In reversed-phase systems (aqueous/organic mixtures) or polar organic modes (100% methanol or acetonitrile), the solvation profile changes dramatically. Highly polar solvents induce measurable swelling of the polymer network. X-ray diffraction data demonstrates that the inter-rod packing distance physically expands as the molar volume and polarity of the absorbed solvent increase.4 Methanol, acting as a small, polar protic solvent, heavily endorses ![][image4]\-![][image4] and hydrogen bond interactions while swelling the matrix, leading to distinct enantiomer elution profiles that differ wildly from normal-phase results.39

To replicate this computationally, the atomistic model must be subjected to a rigorous isothermal-isobaric (NPT) equilibration phase prior to production MD runs. While the initial coordinate files are built using the dry crystallographic dimensions (e.g., 14.6 Å pitch, 18.9 Å packing), the NPT ensemble allows the simulation box vectors to dynamically expand, capturing the exact volumetric swelling induced by the explicit solvent molecules.39 The separation process on these columns is predominately enthalpy-driven, wherein the energetic favorability of the resulting transient inclusion complex (![][image6]) must overcome the entropic penalty (![][image7]) of restricting the analyte's degrees of freedom within the solvated cavity.42

### **Addition of Computational Reconstruction Parameters**

To fulfill the requirement of facilitating accurate atomistic reconstruction, five entirely new categories of structural data have been appended to the dataset:

1. **Helical Pitch (![][image3]):** This value dictates the exact linear translation along the Z-axis required to complete one full 360-degree rotation of the polymer. The value is strictly defined as 14.6 Å for all amylose 4/3 helices and 16.2 Å for all cellulose 3/2 helices.3  
2. **Inter-rod Packing Distance (![][image3]):** This metric defines the exact spatial gap between parallel polymer chains in the crystalline lattice, which is required for defining periodic box vectors. The value is 18.9 Å for the tighter amylose helices and 21.0 Å for the broader cellulose helices.3  
3. **Helical Diameter (![][image3]):** Derived from hard-core spatial modeling and interchain spacing, this value establishes the physical cross-sectional width of the polymer rod.3 It is established at \~14.0 Å for cellulose and \~14.0 to 15.0 Å for the differently packed amylose.  
4. **Mode of Attachment:** This binary classification strictly delineates whether the phase is physically coated (held by non-covalent forces) or covalently immobilized (chemically bonded to the silica), dictating whether topology cross-links are required.5  
5. **Tether/Linkage to Silica:** This explicitly defines the atomistic chemistry bridging the selector to the surface. It details the physical adsorption for coated phases, the diisocyanate/triethoxysilyl chemistry for immobilized polymers, and the specific alkyl silane chemistry for Whelk-O 1\.24

### **Structural Clarifications**

The halogenated variations of the columns (AY, IC, IE, IF, IG, OX, OZ) do not possess independently published full-crystal X-ray datasets due to the difficulty of crystallizing heavily substituted macromolecules. However, in computational practice, they are universally modeled by taking the heavily validated base backbone geometries (ADMPC for amylose, CDMPC for cellulose) and substituting the functional groups *in silico*.3 Therefore, the helical pitch and inter-rod packing distances for the halogenated variants accurately reflect their respective parent backbones. The data for Whelk-O 1 has been explicitly segregated, replacing polymer-specific metrics with "N/A" to prevent automated algorithms from attempting to twist a discrete small molecule into a macroscopic helix.

## **Methodological Workflow for System Assembly**

To transition the data provided in this report into a functional molecular dynamics simulation, a strict methodological hierarchy must be maintained. The reconstruction of polysaccharide stationary phases is notoriously prone to artefactual collapse if the initial coordinates are not rigorously defined. First, the construction of the underlying silica matrix is required. For immobilized and coated phases alike, this involves generating an amorphous silica slab or a specific crystal face (e.g., ![][image2]\-cristobalite (100)) with a highly controlled silanol density, typically approaching 4.5 to 4.7 OH groups per square nanometer. Second, for the covalently immobilized columns (IB, IC, IE, IF, IG), the specific linker chemistry—whether a diisocyanate derivative forming a urethane bond or a triethoxysilyl derivative forming a siloxane bridge—must be grafted onto the silica surface, leaving reactive terminal groups extending into the solvent void. For the Whelk-O 1 system, the entire surface is functionalized with a self-assembled monolayer of the undecenyl or propyl-linked 1-(3,5-dinitrobenzamido)-1,2,3,4-tetrahydrophenanthrene molecules.

Third, the generation of the polymer coordinates must be executed. This is achieved by taking a highly optimized monomeric unit of the specific chiral selector (e.g., the 3-chloro-5-methylphenylcarbamate of glucose for Chiralpak IG) and applying a mathematical rotation matrix. The matrix must enforce the 14.6 Å pitch and 4/3 helical twist for amylose derivatives, or the 16.2 Å pitch and 3/2 helical twist for cellulose derivatives. Fourth, the generated 36-mer or nonamer chains are aligned along the Z-axis of the simulation box. Parallel chains are duplicated and translated along the X and Y axes utilizing the verified inter-rod packing distances—18.9 Å for amylose and 21.0 Å for cellulose—to establish the initial crystalline lattice constraints.

Finally, for the covalently immobilized models, the coordinate files of the polymer network and the functionalized silica slab are merged, and explicit topology bonds are defined connecting a fractional percentage of the polymer's C6 hydroxyls to the silica's linker groups. Following the explicit solvation of the system with the desired mobile phase, the entire assembly must undergo extensive steepest descent energy minimization to resolve any lingering steric clashes, particularly those involving the highly bulky asymmetric phenyl rings on columns like AY, IF, IG, OX, and OZ.

## **Conclusions**

The accurate atomistic reconstruction of chiral stationary phases is an intricate structural puzzle that bridges polymer physics, analytical chemistry, and computational modeling. The analysis confirms that the successful translation of these chromatographic columns into digital space relies unequivocally on preserving the delicate supramolecular geometry inherent to each specific carbohydrate backbone. The physical differences between the tighter 4/3 amylose helix and the broader 3/2 cellulose helix fundamentally define the spatial volume and topographic landscape of the chiral cavity, which in turn dictates the steric feasibility of analyte inclusion and stereoselective resolution. By meticulously incorporating the established crystallographic data—specifically the 14.6 Å pitch and 18.9 Å inter-rod packing of amylose derivatives, versus the 16.2 Å pitch and 21.0 Å inter-rod packing of cellulose derivatives—computational researchers can prevent the artefactual collapse or unnatural expansion of the polymer rods during simulation.

Equally vital to the physical modeling of these systems is the strict distinction between physically coated and covalently immobilized stationary phases. While the local helical geometry remains largely conserved across both manufacturing techniques, the covalent anchoring present in modern immobilized columns chemically restricts full backbone solvation and dictates specific boundary conditions that must be explicitly linked in topological files using the appropriate cross-linking parameters. The integration of advanced electronic modeling, such as the parameterization of sigma-holes on halogenated variants, further refines the accuracy of the modeled interaction energies. Ultimately, by applying the comprehensively verified and expanded structural parameters codified within this dataset, *in silico* investigations will effectively mirror empirical liquid chromatography, bridging the gap between theoretical physical chemistry and practical pharmaceutical enantioseparation.
